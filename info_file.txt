Agents, Account Managers, Consultants — Roles, Onboarding & Qualification
Purpose: This document captures how Agents, Account Managers, and Consultants should sign up, qualify, and operate inside the MCOMMALL (Listeo) marketplace. 
1) High-level summary (one-sentence)
At signup we must identify whether a person is an Agent, Account Manager, or Consultant so the platform can control quality, charge for visibility, route them into training, and show the right features and pay rates.
2) Role hierarchy (Henry’s model)
Agent → entry-level, small, focused tasks. (Lowest gate)
Account Manager → mid-level, coordinates agents, runs campaigns and onboarding. (Higher gate)
Consultant → top-level expert; advisory, audits, strategy. (Highest gate)
This simple ladder allows people to grow: Agent → Account Manager → Consultant.

4) Short card and full description (put these in listing pages and role details modal)
For each role we must include two pieces of copy: a short card (50–80 words) and a full description (200–400 words). Add these exact blocks to the UI so listing pages, dropdowns, and modals can call them.
Agent — Short card (50–80 words)
Agent — Entry-level specialists who complete focused micro-tasks like social post setup, basic email templates, short product descriptions, quick website checks, or small audits. Agents take a short qualification test to appear in listings. They can opt in to be visible for paid assignments and are paid per task. Ideal for fast, low-cost services.
Agent — Full description (200–400 words)
Agents are entry-level service providers focused on quick, repeatable tasks. Their scope includes simple jobs such as setting up a social media post, creating basic email templates, writing short product blurbs, performing a lightweight website check (links, load time hints), or running a small audit. Agents are the platform’s flexible workforce: they complete small assignments quickly and reliably.
Expectations and deliverables:
Clear, short task results (one deliverable per assignment).
Fast turnaround times.
Follow platform templates and checklists for consistency.
Qualification and pay:
Agents must pass a basic qualification quiz to appear in listings.
Agents can toggle a checkbox to apply for paid assignments; paid visibility is reviewed.
Payment model is task-based: fixed fee per micro-service or a small hourly rate.
Support and growth:
Agents can be invited to training modules when they want to upgrade.
High-performing Agents can apply/upgrade to Account Manager after reaching platform milestones.
This role is perfect for people who are building experience, want flexible short work, or provide niche, tactical skills.
Account Manager — Short card (50–80 words)
Account Manager — Mid-level professionals who coordinate onboarding, manage teams of agents, plan and run campaigns, and match businesses to services. Account Managers handle client communications, create campaign plans, and deliver measurable results. They have higher pay, need deeper tests and verification, and can act as project leads across the platform.
Account Manager — Full description (200–400 words)
Account Managers are the project leads and coordinators who ensure client needs are turned into successful campaigns. They onboard businesses, plan campaign schedules, assign tasks to Agents, and handle day-to-day client communications. Their core job is to manage delivery and quality.
Responsibilities:
Client onboarding and intake: gather goals, timelines, assets, and brief the delivery team.
Campaign planning: design the campaign, assign Agents, set deadlines and checkpoints.
Team coordination: supervise Agents, review work, and perform quality checks.
Reporting: deliver simple performance reports and suggested next steps.
Qualification and pay:
Account Managers must pass a deeper quiz and may need verification or a portfolio review.
Payment is higher than Agents; they can charge per-project, per-month retainer, or take a share of campaign revenue.
Why businesses use them:
Account Managers reduce friction: businesses get a single point of contact and a predictable delivery plan.
They are preferred for larger or multi-step projects where coordination matters more than single tasks.
Account Managers form the platform’s mid-tier professionals and are critical for predictable, repeatable business results.
Consultant — Short card (50–80 words)
Consultant — Senior experts who provide business advice, audits, strategic plans, and deep coaching. Consultants can be visible immediately after signup but are flagged for verification. They charge per session or project, deliver high-value recommendations, and are expected to have proven credentials and measurable experience.
Consultant — Full description (200–400 words)
Consultants are senior-level professionals who offer high-value services such as business audits, strategy sessions, marketing plans, financial or operational advice, and advanced coaching. Their work is outcome-focused and typically priced higher than other roles.
Key activities:
Deep discovery and audits: review a business’s current setup and produce a clear list of changes.
Strategy and planning: develop step-by-step plans for growth, product launches, or operational improvements.
Hands-on coaching and session-based work: provide expert time, review deliverables, and help the business implement key changes.
Qualification and verification:
Consultants can create a visible profile immediately, but each profile is flagged and enters a verification queue.
Verification may include document checks, portfolio review, and a live interview or sample session.
Pricing and payment:
Consultants usually charge per session, per project, or via a retainer.
They may also negotiate custom packages for long-term advisory work.
Why businesses choose Consultants:
They want senior guidance and measurable change.
Consultants are used for complex problems where strategic thinking and experienced judgment matter.
Consultants carry the highest trust on the platform, so the platform must verify credentials and protect businesses by ensuring quality control.
5) Training & qualification: questionnaire vs quiz (exact steps)
Henry asked for a two-part approach: a Questionnaire for quick tagging and a Quismatic quiz for real grading. Build both in Canvas as separate pages.
A. Questionnaire (8–12 short questions)
Purpose: quick surface data to tag skills, experience levels, and availability. These answers create quick filters and populate the user’s profile tags.
Suggested questions (multi-choice or short select):
Which role are you applying for? (Agent / Account Manager / Consultant)
Pick your top 2–3 skills. (multi-select from the pre-defined list)
How many years of experience do you have in these skills? (0–1 / 1–3 / 3–5 / 5+)
What types of services have you delivered? (social posts / emails / copy / SEO / ads / design)
What is your typical turnaround time for small tasks? (<24h / 24–72h / 3–7 days)
Are you available for immediate paid assignments? (Yes / No)
Do you have a portfolio link or sample work? (Yes — provide link / No)
Do you accept fixed-price micro-tasks? (Yes / No)
Preferred payment currency? (dropdown)
Languages you speak fluently (multi-select)
Result: Store answers as skill tags and quick filters on the profile (e.g., "social-posts", "email-marketing", "fast-turnaround").
B. Quismatic Quiz (10–20 scored questions)
Purpose: a scored competency test that decides whether the person can be visible for paid assignments, must take training, or is ready for higher roles.
Design notes for the quiz engine:
Each question has a weight and correct answer(s).
Mix practical multiple-choice and scenario questions (not only theory).
Randomize question order on retakes.
Show overall score and band at the end.
Allow a short explanation for each correct answer in the review screen.
Provide time limits per question or for the whole quiz (optional).
Example quiz topics and sample questions:
Social media setup and posting — e.g., "You need to schedule 5 posts for a launch. Which elements must be included in each post?" (options)
Email marketing basics — e.g., "What is a good subject line strategy for a promotional email?" (options)
Writing short product descriptions — e.g., "Which sentence best balances features and benefit?" (choose best)
Using ChatGPT for content — e.g., "Which prompt results in a better product blurb?" (choose best)
Simple website checks — e.g., "You find a broken image and slow load time on a product page; which steps do you report?" (scenario)
Sample scoring bands (customize later):
85–100%: Pass — Eligible for Consultant / Account Manager application (if other checks pass)
70–84%: Pass — Eligible for Account Manager / Agent with higher visibility
50–69%: Partial — Eligible for Agent-level tasks; recommended training modules
0–49%: Fail — Must complete suggested training before paid visibility
Outcome actions after quiz:
Create a score badge on the profile (e.g., "Quismatic: 78% — Account Manager candidate").
If pass band reached: show "Apply for paid visibility" and route to a verification step.
If fail or partial: show recommended training modules and an option to retake the quiz after completing training.

Roles, Onboarding & Qualification
Goal


=========================================================================================================
Make it easy for people to sign up as an Agent, Account Manager, or Consultant, capture their skills, and route them into the right training and verification flows. The form must be made from small reusable blocks (components) so designers and developers can reuse them in different pages.
Step 1 — Build the role hierarchy content
Create three role descriptions in the CMS: Agent, Account Manager, Consultant.
For each role add two content blocks:
Short card (50–80 words) to use on listing pages and dropdowns.
Full description (200–400 words) to use in a role-details modal or profile page.
Add a simple ladder graphic showing: Agent → Account Manager → Consultant and place this on the provider dashboard and on the signup help page.
Make sure the ladder text explains that people can grow from Agent up to Consultant and show example milestones (quiz score, jobs completed, ratings).
Step 2 — Make the Role selection block (component)
Create a dropdown component titled Which role are you applying for?
Options: Agent, Account Manager, Consultant.
When a user selects Consultant:
Allow user to continue and create a basic profile immediately.
Set a hidden field profile_status = "pending_verification" so admins can review later.
On selection change, save the selection to the user profile draft so they can finish the form later.
Reuse this component in signup, profile edit, and admin impersonation pages.
Step 3 — Build the Short skills selector (component)
Create a chip-based multi-select component labeled Pick your top 2–3 skills.
Limit selection to 2–3 choices. If user tries to pick more, show a short helper message: "Pick up to 2–3 top skills."
Use a default skill list that admins can edit in the CMS. Start with these skills:
Social media setup & posting
Email marketing basics
Short product descriptions / copywriting
ChatGPT / AI content generation
Simple website checks (speed, links, SEO basics)
Basic graphic design (banners, thumbnails)
Customer messaging & support
Local SEO / listings
Google Ads basics
Facebook ads basics
When user picks skills, save them as tags on the profile. Also store a primary_skill (first chosen) for quick search boosts.
Display the skills on the public profile as chips.
Step 4 — Add the Paid-assignment visibility checkbox (component)
Create a checkbox labeled I want to apply to be visible for paid assignments.
If checked, create a paid_visibility_request record in the system and route the user to the verification flow.
Show a small tooltip: "If approved, your profile will be shown to businesses hiring paid suppliers. Approval may include a short review."
Save the checkbox state to the user draft and show status in the provider dashboard (Requested / Approved / Denied).
Step 5 — Build Quick profile fields (component group)
Create a reusable block with these short fields:
Full name / Business name (single field or two fields)
Email / Phone (validated)
Short bio (50–120 characters) — show a character counter
Hourly or per-session rate (optional) — currency selector
Country / Timezone / Languages (multi-select)
Portfolio link / sample work (URL)
Upload ID / verification docs (file input) — hidden unless user starts verification or selects paid assignments
Implementation notes:
Keep the initial signup minimal — ask only name, email, role, and skills.
Allow users to complete the rest of the quick profile after signup (profile completion flow).
Use client-side validation for common errors (invalid URL, missing timezone).
Step 6 — CTA buttons and routing
Create three CTA buttons and wire them to the right flows:
Apply to be an Agent: route to Questionnaire page (quick) then Quismatic quiz
Apply to be an Account Manager: route to Questionnaire then Quismatic quiz
Apply to be a Consultant: allow immediate profile creation (flag pending_verification) then route to Questionnaire + Quismatic quiz for verification
UX tips:
On each CTA, show a small progress bar: "Step 1 of 3: Role selection → Questionnaire → Quiz → Outcome"
Allow users to save progress and return later.
Step 7 — Why consultants are flagged
Consultants can create a visible profile immediately so businesses can find and contact them.
Set profile_status = "pending_verification" and prevent assignment matching until verification is complete.
Admins must review consultant uploads: portfolio, credentials, and optionally request a sample session.
Make the verification step a clear button in the admin queue: Approve / Request more info / Deny.
Step 8 — Small copy & helper text for each block
Add short helper text under each component explaining why it matters:
Role dropdown: "Choose the role you will perform on the platform. This affects what work you can get."
Skills selector: "Pick 2–3 skill areas you are best at. Keep it short so businesses see your strengths."
Paid checkbox: "Apply to be visible for paid jobs — approval may take up to X days."
Portfolio: "Add a link so businesses can see your previous work."
Consultant flag: "Consultant profiles are checked by our team before offering paid jobs."
Step 9 — Acceptance & validation rules
Require role + at least 1 skill to proceed.
Require email + name for account creation.
If the paid-assignment checkbox is checked, require portfolio link or at least one sample upload before allowing submission.
Limit the skills to 2–3 strictly on the client-side; enforce on the backend as well.
Step 10 — Admin & developer notes
Make skill list editable in admin UI and versioned.
Store skill tags in a normalized table for fast search.
Log all paid-visibility requests with timestamps and admin IDs who reviewed them.
Add API endpoints for: create profile, update profile, submit paid-visibility, fetch skill list, submit verification docs.
Step 11 — Testing checklist
 Signup: role selection saves correctly for all three roles
 Skills selector enforces 2–3 limit on client and server
 Paid-assignment checkbox creates request and shows in admin queue
 Consultant flag sets pending_verification and blocks paid assignment matching
 CTA buttons route to correct flows
 Form saves drafts and resumes
 File uploads secured and accessible to admin only
End of step-by-step note. Use these blocks to build the signup flow in Canvas. Designers can copy the short helper text and label copy directly into UI mockups. Developers can use the admin & developer notes to set up the backend and API endpoints.
